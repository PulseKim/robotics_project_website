Discovering Diverse Motion Skills from Reference Motions
for Humanoid Robots

Sun Woo Kim

Simon Fraser University · CMPT 720 Course Project · 2026

Overview

We study how to learn diverse and reusable motor skills for humanoid robots from human reference motions. Our method follows a three-stage pipeline. First, we retarget motion capture data to the target robot through motion optimization. Second, we learn a latent-conditioned low-level policy with adversarial imitation and skill discovery. Third, we continue training with a skill discovery objective to expand the learned behavior space beyond the reference distribution.

We evaluate the learned skills on the Unitree G1 humanoid in Isaac Gym and test whether they can be reused through a hierarchical controller on downstream tasks such as steering and goal-directed navigation.

Method

Pipeline figure goes here

Stage 1: Retargeting

We align the source and target skeletons in T-pose and retarget the source motion to the robot by solving a motion optimization problem with positional, rotational, temporal, and motion plausibility constraints.

Stage 2: Reusable Skill Learning

We train a latent-conditioned low-level policy by combining adversarial imitation with skill discovery, following prior work on ASE-style skill learning.

Stage 3: Exploration

Starting from the stage-two policy, we optimize only the skill discovery objective while using the discriminator as a mask so that exploration does not drift too far from plausible motion.

Experimental Setup

All experiments are conducted on the Unitree G1 humanoid in Isaac Gym. We use the MimicKit dataset as the source of reference motions. Policies are trained with PPO using 4096 parallel environments on a single NVIDIA H100 GPU. The simulator runs at 120 Hz and the control frequency is 30 Hz.

Conclusion

We presented a three-stage framework for learning diverse and reusable motion skills from reference motions for humanoid robots. Our experiments show that the final exploration stage can discover behaviors beyond the original dataset, although controllability and skill collapse remain important limitations for future work.
